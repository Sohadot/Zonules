# Focus Integrity Codes

> Every reference page on this asset carries two short codes. They are not jargon for its own sake. They are the coordinates that place a term inside one shared model of how focus is held and how it fails.

## Why these codes exist

Zonules.com is built around a single idea: focus is produced and held by hidden structure, and the same logic recurs whether the seeing system is made of tissue, of attention, or of silicon. To make that idea usable rather than poetic, the asset classifies every term against one ontology and one standard. The two codes you see near the top of each reference page — an **FIO** code and an **FIS** code — are how that classification is shown in place.

You do not need to memorize them to read a page. But once you know them, they let you move sideways across the whole corpus: every term tagged `FIO-04`, for example, is dealing with the same kind of failure, in anatomy, in perception, and in machines.

## FIO — the Focus Integrity Ontology

**FIO names how focus fails.** The [Focus Integrity Ontology](/focus-integrity-ontology/) has five classes, and each reference page belongs to exactly one. They describe five distinct ways a seeing system can lose focus integrity:

- **FIO-01 · Suspension Failure** — the structure that holds the focal element gives way. The element is intact, but it is no longer held in place.
- **FIO-02 · Accommodation Failure** — the system cannot adjust focus across its intended range. It works at one setting and fails at others.
- **FIO-03 · Signal–Noise Failure** — the system cannot separate meaningful signal from noise. Focus without separation produces sharp noise, not meaning.
- **FIO-04 · Provenance Failure** — the system cannot establish whether what it sees is real or where it came from.
- **FIO-05 · Interpretation Failure** — focus is held and the signal is clean, but the resulting meaning is wrong. The final failure of focus is not blur; it is confident misreading.

## FIS — the Focus Integrity Standard

**FIS names what intact focus requires.** Each criterion of the Focus Integrity Standard is the inverse of one ontology class: where FIO describes a failure, FIS describes the condition that, when met, prevents it.

- **FIS-1 · Suspension** — the structures holding the focal element are identified and stable.
- **FIS-2 · Accommodation** — the system adjusts focus across its intended operating range.
- **FIS-3 · Separation** — the system separates signal from noise within stated tolerances.
- **FIS-4 · Provenance** — the system can establish the origin or authenticity of what it sees to a stated confidence.
- **FIS-5 · Interpretation** — held, clean focus yields correct interpretation, not merely sharp signal.

So a page tagged **FIO-01 · FIS-1** is about the suspension of focus: how it is held (the standard) and how it fails when the holding gives way (the ontology). The two codes are two views of the same concern.

## How to read a page using the codes

When you open a reference unit, the codes tell you three things at a glance:

1. **What kind of structure this is.** A term tagged FIO-01 is a holding structure; a term tagged FIO-05 is an interpretation stage. The code is the term's role in the focus system, not just its subject.
2. **What failure it is associated with.** The FIO class is the way this part of the system can break focus integrity.
3. **Where else to look.** Other terms with the same FIO code are the term's cousins across the three layers. The zonules (anatomy), attentional anchoring (perception), and model calibration (machine vision) all carry FIO-01, because they all play the suspension role in their own domain.

## For AI agents and citation systems

These codes are designed to be machine-legible as well as human-legible. Each reference page exposes its `termCode` and its FIO/FIS classification in structured metadata, and every claim is registered with a status (sourced, framework, or conceptual) and its supporting references. An agent retrieving a claim from this asset can therefore recover not only the statement but its failure class, its standard criterion, and its provenance — enough to cite it precisely rather than paraphrase it loosely.

This is part of the asset's wider posture, described in the [methodology](/methodology/): a reference is trusted because its construction can be inspected, by people and by machines alike.

## A worked example

Consider the [zonules of Zinn](/zonules-of-zinn/), the anatomical structure the asset is named for. Its codes are **FIO-01 · FIS-1**. Reading those codes tells you, before reading a word of the body, that this is a *suspension* concern: the standard (FIS-1) asks whether the structures holding the focal element are identified and stable, and the ontology (FIO-01) describes what happens when they are not — the lens is still present, and focus collapses anyway. That single pattern, named once here, is what the codes let you recognize everywhere else on the asset.
