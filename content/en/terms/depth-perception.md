# Depth Perception

> Two flat images. One world in three dimensions. Depth perception is not the reception of space — it is the construction of it.

## Definition

**Depth perception** is the visual ability to perceive the relative distance of objects in three-dimensional space from the two-dimensional images projected onto the retinae. [CLM-084] The world is three-dimensional; the retina is a curved two-dimensional surface. Depth is not received — it is inferred, from cues distributed across the visual input.

## Scientific Grounding

**Binocular cues** arise from the fact that the two eyes are horizontally separated and therefore receive slightly different images of the same scene. **Binocular disparity** — the magnitude of this difference for any given point — is the primary cue for depth at close range; the brain resolves disparity into a perception of depth through **stereopsis**. [CLM-085] Convergence — the inward rotation of both eyes toward a close object — provides additional proprioceptive depth information.

**Monocular depth cues** are available from a single eye's image and include linear perspective (parallel lines converging in the distance), familiar size (known objects appearing smaller when further away), occlusion (closer objects overlapping farther ones), and motion parallax (near objects moving faster across the visual field than far objects during movement). [CLM-086] Monocular cues extend the range of depth perception beyond the distances where binocular disparity is useful.

*Sources: sensation and perception and vision science references (SRC-005, SRC-006). See the source registry for classification.*

## Interpretation as Reconstruction

Depth perception is a problem of interpretation in the focus-integrity sense. [CLM-087] The visual system does not receive depth; it constructs it. A 2D retinal image is consistent with infinitely many 3D scenes — the perceptual system selects one construction as the most probable, guided by the available cues. When those cues conflict, are absent, or are misread, the constructed depth is wrong. The visual scene is present and unambiguous to the photoreceptors, but the spatial interpretation is incorrect.

This is a characteristically interpretive failure: the signal is received correctly, but the meaning attributed to it — in this case, the spatial layout of the world — is wrong.

## Relationship to the Governing Thesis

At Layer 01, the optical system delivers a 2D focused image to the retina. At Layer 02, the perceptual system must interpret that image as a 3D world — an act of inference built on hidden structural mechanisms. The cues that support depth perception are, in most circumstances, invisible to the perceiver: perspective, disparity, and parallax are not consciously accessed but structurally processed. The hidden structure holds the 3D interpretation together, just as hidden structural tension holds focal integrity in the eye.
