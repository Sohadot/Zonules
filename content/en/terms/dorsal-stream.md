# Dorsal Stream

> The pathway for where things are and how to act.

## Definition

The dorsal stream is the cortical visual pathway running from the visual cortex toward the parietal lobe, associated with spatial location and visually guided action. [CLM-900]

## Scientific Grounding

It is often summarized as the where, or how, pathway. [CLM-901] It draws heavily on fast magnocellular input. [CLM-902]

*Sources: SRC-009, SRC-013. See the source registry for classification.*

## Anatomical Role

The dorsal stream is an interpretation-layer route: it reads the scene for where things are and how to act on them. [CLM-903]

## Mechanism

The dorsal stream projects from primary visual cortex through parietal areas, carrying fast, motion-sensitive magnocellular input used to localize objects and guide movement. [CLM-1325]

## Why It Matters

It supplies the visual control of action — reaching, grasping, and navigating — often without conscious awareness of the computations involved. [CLM-1326] In the thesis it is an interpretation route specialized for where things are and how to act on them. [CLM-1327]

## Failure Mode

Parietal damage to the dorsal stream can impair reaching toward or navigating around objects that the person can still recognize, dissociating action from identity. [CLM-1328]

## Human–Machine Bridge

Robotics and visual-SLAM systems implement a dorsal-like function, using vision for localization and action separately from the recognition that names what is seen. [CLM-1329]

## Common Misunderstanding

The dorsal stream is often labeled simply the where pathway; it is better understood as the how pathway, computing vision for action rather than mere location. [CLM-1330]
