Fovea Centralis

Every optical element in the eye exists to deliver one clean signal to this single point.

Definition

The fovea centralis is a shallow depression at the centre of the macula, approximately 1.5 mm in diameter, containing the highest concentration of cone photoreceptors in the eye.

Scientific Grounding

At the foveal centre, overlying retinal layers are displaced laterally, leaving only cone photoreceptors to receive the focused image with minimal optical scatter. The fovea contains no rod photoreceptors; it is entirely cone-mediated, conferring high-acuity, colour-sensitive vision in the central visual field.

*Sources: SRC-001, SRC-003. See the source registry for classification.*

Structural Role

The fovea is the anatomical zero-point of focus integrity (FIO-03): every optical element exists to deliver a clean signal to this location.

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

Fovea Centralis is one instance of the asset's governing claim: that focus is produced and held by structure that is not itself visible in the result. Its place in the focus-integrity ontology is fixed as a FIO-03 (Signal–Noise) concern, the same failure class whether the seeing system is made of tissue, attention, or silicon.
