Illusory Contours

An edge seen clearly where no edge exists.

Definition

Illusory contours are perceived edges that appear where no luminance boundary exists in the image, as in the Kanizsa triangle.

Scientific Grounding

The visual system infers a continuous edge and an occluding surface from the arrangement of surrounding inducers. The illusory surface is often seen as brighter than its physically identical background.

*Sources: SRC-006, SRC-005. See the source registry for classification.*

Perceptual Role

Illusory contours are an interpretation-layer construction — a boundary asserted by the percept that the signal does not contain.

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

Illusory Contours is one instance of the asset's governing claim: that focus is produced and held by structure that is not itself visible in the result. Its place in the focus-integrity ontology is fixed as an FIO-05 — Interpretation concern, the same failure class whether the seeing system is made of tissue, attention, or silicon.
