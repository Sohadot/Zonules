Perceptual Constancy

The world changes constantly as you move through it. The perceptual system holds it still. That stability is not naive — it is inference.

Definition

Perceptual constancy is the tendency of the perceptual system to maintain a stable representation of an object despite variations in viewing angle, distance, illumination, or surrounding context. It is what allows you to recognize a chair as the same chair whether you are standing, seated, near, or far — despite the fact that the sensory image on your retina changes continuously.

Scientific Grounding

Classical forms of perceptual constancy include size constancy (perceived size remains stable across changes in viewing distance), shape constancy (perceived shape remains stable across changes in viewing angle), and lightness constancy (perceived surface reflectance remains stable across changes in illumination). Each represents the perceptual system maintaining a property of the world despite the fact that the sensory signal encoding it is variable.

Constancy is not passive. It depends on the visual system's ability to discount the conditions of observation — to infer what is constant in the world by reasoning about what varies in the signal. When you perceive a white sheet in dim light as white rather than grey, you are performing an implicit inference about illumination. This is perceptual inference: an active construction, not a readout.

Constancy as Interpretation Integrity

Perceptual constancy is the perceptual analogue of interpretation integrity — FIS-5 in the Focus Integrity Standard. A perceptual system with intact constancy holds a stable interpretation of reality across varying conditions, which is precisely what it means for interpretation focus to be intact.

Constancy failure is interpretive failure: the percept is present, the sensory input is arriving, but the system draws the wrong conclusion about what is out there. A shadow that is mistaken for a painted surface, a receding object that appears to shrink as a property of the object rather than of viewing distance — these are failures of the inferential mechanism that produces constancy.

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

The governing thesis is that focus is produced by hidden structural tension. At Layer 02, the structural tension that produces stable perception is the inferential mechanism of constancy: a continuous, invisible process of compensating for the conditions of observation so that the interpreted world does not flutter with every change in the signal. Like the zonules, it is not noticed when it works.

Relationship to Machine Vision (Layer 03)

In machine vision, the analogue of perceptual constancy is robustness to distribution shift: the ability of a model to maintain correct interpretation across conditions that differ from those of training. A model that fails outside its training distribution has lost its interpretive constancy. This is a conceptual mapping, marked as internal framework language.
