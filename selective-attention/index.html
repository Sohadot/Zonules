Selective Attention

The zonules hold the lens. Attention holds the target. Both are suspension systems; only one is made of tissue.

Definition

Selective attention is the mechanism by which the visual system prioritizes some stimuli for detailed processing while suppressing others. It is the perceptual layer's answer to the question the zonules answer in anatomy: what holds focus in place?

Scientific Grounding

Attention can be directed voluntarily (top-down, by goals) or captured involuntarily (bottom-up, by salient stimuli). Because attentional capacity is limited, not all of the visual field can be processed in detail at once — the system must choose where focus is held.

*Sources: standard academic reference in sensation and perception (SRC-005).*

System Role

Selective attention is the perceptual suspension mechanism. It holds a focal target steady against competing stimuli the way the zonules hold the lens steady against the forces inside the eye. When attention is captured, divided, or overloaded, the focal target is present but no longer held — the perceptual form of suspension failure.

Relationship to Anatomy (Layer 01)

The parallel to the zonules of Zinn is structural, not metaphorical decoration: both are systems whose only job is to *hold* a focal element so that focus can occur. Neither produces the image; both make it stable.

Relationship to Machine Vision (Layer 03)

In machine perception, attention mechanisms gate which regions a model processes in detail — the engineered analogue of selective attention, and subject to the same suspension-failure logic when grounding drifts.

Common Confusions

  • Attention vs. perception. Perception is the whole construction of meaning; attention is the prioritization that decides what gets constructed in detail.
  • Looking vs. attending. The eyes can be pointed at something the mind is not attending to. Gaze is not attention.
