Attention Capture

The eye goes where salience commands. The question is whether salience was earned by the signal or engineered by the noise.

Definition

Attention capture is the involuntary, stimulus-driven reorientation of visual attention toward a salient event in the visual field, regardless of current task goals or intentions. A salient stimulus — one that differs markedly from its surround in luminance, colour, motion, or onset — reflexively recruits attentional resources without requiring deliberate direction.

Scientific Grounding

The literature distinguishes two modes of attentional control. Goal-directed (endogenous) attention is voluntarily directed toward task-relevant targets; it is under observer control and operates slowly. Stimulus-driven (exogenous) attention is reflexively redirected by salient events in the periphery; it operates rapidly (100–200 ms) and automatically, before the observer can apply task goals.

Attention capture by abrupt onsets — the sudden appearance of a new object — is among the most robust phenomena in visual attention research. High-contrast stimuli, looming objects, and socially significant stimuli (faces, emotional expressions) are strong capture agents. Critically, capture can occur even when observers are explicitly instructed to ignore the capturing stimulus, demonstrating the automatic, pre-volitional character of the mechanism. The salience map — a spatial representation combining feature contrast across multiple dimensions — is the computational construct most commonly used to predict capture likelihood at any scene location.

Sources: SRC-005, SRC-006. See source registry.

Capture as Signal-Noise Problem

Attention capture is fundamentally a signal-to-noise problem at the attentional level. When a salient but task-irrelevant stimulus captures attention, it functions as noise: it occupies attentional resources that would otherwise be directed at the target signal. The severity of interference depends on the relative salience of the distractor compared to the target, the observer's current task, and the perceptual load conditions of the scene. Deliberately engineered capture — attention pulled to an element by design rather than by relevance — is the attentional analogue of introducing artificial noise into a signal channel.

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

In the Focus Integrity framework, attention capture is the perceptual mechanism by which the signal-to-noise ratio fails at the attentional level even when the optical signal is clean. A well-captured image that contains a salient, task-irrelevant element will systematically pull focus away from the relevant content — a FIO-03 failure that requires deliberate design countermeasures, not optical correction.
