Binocular Vision

Two eyes see two slightly different images. The visual system interprets that difference as depth — constructing a three-dimensional spatial model from the disparity between two two-dimensional inputs. This is not duplication; it is a richer interpretation made possible by redundancy.

Definition

Binocular vision is the integration of visual input from two eyes into a single, spatially coherent percept. Because the eyes are laterally separated, each receives a slightly different view of the scene. The visual cortex combines these two inputs, and the difference between them — binocular disparity — is computed as a signal for three-dimensional depth. The result is depth perception through stereopsis, an interpretation unavailable to either eye alone. Binocular integration occurs primarily in the primary visual cortex, which contains neurons driven by input from both eyes.

Scientific Grounding

Binocular single vision requires that the two retinal images be fused into one percept rather than perceived as double. This fusion requires the two eyes to point at the same location in space (alignment) and for the brain to match corresponding regions of the two images. Failures of this correspondence produce diplopia (double vision) or suppression of one eye’s input.

The oculomotor system maintains binocular alignment dynamically through vergence eye movements: converging for near targets, diverging for far ones. This is an accommodation process at the level of eye position, analogous to lens accommodation at the level of focus.

Binocular rivalry occurs when the two eyes receive incompatible images; the brain alternates between them rather than fusing, showing that the perceptual system actively selects an interpretation rather than passively recording both inputs.

Sources: SRC-001, SRC-005, SRC-006. See the source registry for classification.

Integration as Interpretation

Binocular vision is an interpretation problem (FIO-05): the visual system receives two signals and must compute from their difference a representation that neither signal contains — the depth dimension of space. Failure at integration produces not just less information but a qualitatively wrong spatial model.

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

Binocular vision shows that the visual system produces more than what either sensor delivers: by combining two inputs, it constructs a richer interpretation. This is the anatomical instance of interpretation integrity — the capacity to construct the correct meaning from available signals, including the depth dimension of a scene.

Related Terms

  • Visual cortex — where binocular signals are combined and disparity is computed
  • Depth perception — the primary outcome of binocular disparity processing
  • Retina — the source of the two separate images that are integrated
  • Macula — the high-acuity zone where binocular fusion matters most
  • Oculomotor system — maintains the alignment required for binocular fusion
  • Fovea centralis — the point on each retina where binocular fixation is sharpest
