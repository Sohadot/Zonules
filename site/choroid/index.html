Choroid

The retina can only convert light into signals if it is alive. The choroid is the vascular infrastructure that keeps it alive — the hidden supply system behind the visual surface.

Definition

The choroid is the highly vascular layer of the posterior eye wall, lying between the sclera and the retina. It supplies oxygen and nutrients to the outer layers of the retina, including the photoreceptors and retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), which are avascular and depend entirely on choroidal blood flow for their metabolic needs. The choroid also absorbs excess light that passes through the retina, reducing intraocular scatter. The macular region of the choroid (the choroicapillaris) has the highest blood flow per unit tissue of any structure in the body, reflecting the photoreceptors’ extraordinary metabolic demand.

Scientific Grounding

Choroidal neovascularisation — abnormal growth of new blood vessels from the choroid into the subretinal space — is the primary mechanism of vision loss in wet age-related macular degeneration (AMD), the leading cause of severe central vision loss in older adults in high-income countries. The choroid’s integrity is therefore directly tied to the integrity of the macula and the photoreceptors it supports.

The choroid also plays a role in controlling axial eye length during development; choroidal thickness and blood flow are part of the feedback mechanism that governs emmetropisation (the process by which the eye grows to achieve focused vision).

Sources: SRC-001, SRC-003. See the source registry for classification.

Metabolic Suspension

The choroid’s role is metabolic suspension: it sustains the living system that performs phototransduction. Just as the zonules hold the lens in mechanical position, the choroid holds the retina in metabolic readiness. Without it, the retina’s physical structure may remain intact but its signal-producing function collapses.

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

The choroid illustrates that focus integrity has a metabolic dimension. A perfectly formed, precisely focused image reaching a metabolically compromised retina cannot be transduced into signals. Structural focus is necessary but not sufficient: the signal-producing machinery must also be sustained.

Related Terms

  • Retina — the tissue the choroid nourishes
  • Sclera — the outer shell adjacent to the choroid
  • Macula — the high-demand zone served by the choriocapillaris
  • Photoreceptors — the cells dependent on choroidal oxygen and nutrient supply
  • Vitreous humor — the adjacent gel on the inner side of the retina
