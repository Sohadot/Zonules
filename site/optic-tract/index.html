Optic Tract

After the chiasm sorts the signal, the optic tract carries it to the brain’s first relay station. It is the bridge between the routing junction and the thalamic gateway — a conduit whose integrity the visual system cannot substitute.

Definition

The optic tract is the neural pathway that carries visual signals from the optic chiasm to the lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) of the thalamus. Each optic tract contains fibres from the temporal retina of the ipsilateral eye and the nasal retina of the contralateral eye — that is, fibres carrying signals from the same half of the visual field, now sorted by the chiasm. Damage to an optic tract produces a contralateral homonymous hemianopia: loss of vision in the same half of the visual field from both eyes.

Scientific Grounding

The optic tract is a white matter pathway in the brain, not a peripheral nerve. Its fibres are myelinated axons of retinal ganglion cells. Unlike peripheral nerves, these central axons do not regenerate after injury; damage is permanent. The tract wraps around the cerebral peduncle on each side before reaching the LGN at the posterior thalamus.

The topographic organisation of the retina — the retinotopic map — is preserved through the optic tract. Neighbouring points in the visual field are carried by neighbouring fibres, maintaining the spatial structure of the visual image as a property of the pathway itself.

Sources: SRC-001, SRC-002. See the source registry for classification.

Structural Conduit Integrity

The optic tract illustrates a general principle of focus integrity: the signal can be perfectly formed at the retina and accurately routed at the chiasm, yet be lost entirely if the conduit between them fails. The visual system has no redundant pathway for this segment. Suspension integrity at this level is binary: the tract is intact or it is not.

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

The optic tract is a suspension element in the same sense as the zonules: a structural conduit whose function is invisible when intact and catastrophic when damaged. Both hold the signal’s path in place by structural integrity alone.
