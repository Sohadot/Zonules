Perceptual Error

A perceptual error is not a malfunction. It is the correct output of a heuristic operating outside its domain.

Definition

Perceptual error is the systematic or random discrepancy between the physical properties of a stimulus and the observer's subjective interpretation of it. Systematic perceptual errors arise from biases built into the visual system's heuristics and inference mechanisms; they are predictable, direction-specific, and consistent across observers. Random perceptual errors arise from noise in neural processing and vary unpredictably across trials.

Scientific Grounding

Optical illusions are the canonical demonstration of systematic perceptual error. The Müller-Lyer illusion (lines of equal length appear different due to arrow-head terminators), the Ponzo illusion (converging lines produce apparent size differences), and the Ebbinghaus illusion (surrounding context distorts perceived size) all share the same structure: the visual system's contextual heuristics, normally adaptive, produce a demonstrably incorrect output when applied to the illusion figure.

Crucially, perceptual errors of this kind are not eliminated by knowing they are illusions. The compelling nature of systematic errors demonstrates that the interpretation mechanism is largely automatic and pre-volitional — knowledge of the error does not prevent it. This distinguishes perceptual error from judgement error: one can know a line looks longer while knowing it is not. The error occurs at the perceptual, not the propositional, level.

Context effects, lightness constancy failures, motion aftereffects, and face adaptation aftereffects are all manifestations of the same class of mechanism — a visual system adapted to natural statistics of the world producing systematic biases when stimuli depart from those statistics.

Sources: SRC-005, SRC-006. See source registry.

Systematic vs Random Error

The distinction between systematic and random error is important for Focus Integrity analysis. Systematic errors are attributable to the heuristics of the visual system and can, in principle, be characterised and corrected for at the population level. Random errors are noise-driven and affect individual percepts unpredictably. The FIO-05 class covers systematic interpretation errors — cases where a well-transmitted signal is assigned an incorrect meaning not by chance but by the structure of the inference mechanism.

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

Within Focus Integrity, perceptual error is the measurable evidence that FIO-05 has occurred. It is not sufficient to ensure that a signal was transmitted without distortion; it is necessary to ensure that the interpretation of that signal is defended against the known systematic biases of the perceptual system. This applies with equal force to human observers and to machine vision systems — both are subject to systematic errors arising from their training on the statistics of natural images.
