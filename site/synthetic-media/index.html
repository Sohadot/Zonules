Synthetic Media

A synthetic image may be optically perfect. That is precisely what makes it a provenance problem, not an optical one.

Definition

Synthetic media is any image, video, or audio content that has been generated or substantially modified by machine learning systems rather than directly captured from the physical world. It encompasses outputs of generative adversarial networks, diffusion models, neural radiance field renderings, and text-to-image synthesis systems — the full class of machine-generated visual content.

Scientific Grounding

The distinguishing characteristic of modern synthetic media is photorealism: outputs from state-of-the-art generative systems are frequently indistinguishable from authentic photography by human observers, and detection systems based on pixel-level forensics face an adversarial arms race in which generative systems improve faster than detectors. This renders pixel-level authenticity assessment unreliable as a primary provenance strategy.

Content provenance standards — notably the C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) Technical Specification — address this by attaching cryptographically anchored metadata to images at the point of capture or generation. This metadata records the origin of the image (camera, software, generative model) and a tamper-evident log of subsequent modifications. Verification of this metadata chain allows a receiver to distinguish authenticated-capture from authenticated-generation, even when the pixel content is visually indistinguishable.

The absence of provenance metadata does not imply manipulation; it implies unknown provenance. This asymmetry is important: a clean, unfalsified capture without C2PA credentials is indistinguishable from a high-quality synthetic image without credentials. Provenance infrastructure is necessary precisely because pixel content alone cannot bear the weight of origin claims.

Sources: SRC-007, SRC-008. See source registry.

The Provenance Problem

Synthetic media makes the provenance problem categorical rather than marginal. Previously, image authenticity could be contested on the margins — was this image manipulated? Now it is categorical — was this image captured at all? The FIO-04 Provenance failure class exists precisely for this condition: when the chain of custody between a visual asset and the world it purports to represent is broken or unverifiable.

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

Zonules.com treats synthetic media as the defining provenance challenge of contemporary visual culture. The Focus Integrity Standard's FIS-4 Provenance criterion requires that high-stakes visual assets carry verifiable origin metadata — not because pixels can be trusted to reveal their own history, but because they cannot. Provenance is the record that makes a visual signal trustworthy independent of its content.
