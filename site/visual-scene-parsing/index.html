Visual Scene Parsing

To look at a scene is to impose a grammar on it. The grammar comes from the observer, not from the light.

Definition

Visual scene parsing is the process by which the visual system segments a complex input into distinct regions, assigns identities to those regions, and organises them into a structured spatial representation specifying the relationships between elements. It encompasses figure-ground segregation, object segmentation, scene categorisation, and spatial layout recovery — all occurring simultaneously and interactively in natural viewing.

Scientific Grounding

Gist perception is the entry point of scene parsing: within the first 100 ms of viewing, the visual system extracts a global, holistic summary of the scene's category (indoor/outdoor, urban/natural, presence of animate objects) and dominant spatial layout. This rapid categorisation precedes detailed object-level identification and constrains it — knowledge that a scene is a kitchen influences where the system expects to find objects and how it parses boundaries.

Segmentation proceeds by grouping image regions that share properties (colour, texture, motion, common fate) and separating regions that differ across these dimensions. Figure-ground assignment determines which regions are perceived as objects against a ground. Depth ordering specifies which objects occlude which. These operations are not sequential but parallel and mutually constraining, with scene-level representations feeding back to influence region-level segmentation.

Scene schemas — stored templates of expected scene configurations — strongly influence parsing by generating predictions about likely object locations. A chair is expected at floor level in an office scene; detecting it at ceiling level requires resolution of the resulting schema conflict. Schema violation produces slower and less accurate parsing than schema-consistent configurations.

Sources: SRC-005, SRC-006. See source registry.

Parsing as Interpretation

Visual scene parsing is fundamentally an interpretive act. The same physical image can be parsed differently by observers with different schemas, expectations, or expertise. Expert radiologists parse a chest X-ray differently from a novice observer; a trained wildlife photographer parses a natural scene differently from an untrained viewer. These differences are not optical — the image is identical — they are interpretation differences arising from different knowledge structures applied during the parsing process.

Layer Classification

  • FIS Criterion: FIS-5 Interpretation — requires that parsing-level claims (what constitutes figure, ground, subject, background) be explicitly documented rather than assumed

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

Zonules.com treats visual scene parsing as the point at which a clean optical signal is converted into a structured narrative — and where the Focus Integrity risk shifts entirely from optical to interpretive. A well-transmitted image can be parsed into entirely different scenes by observers with different schemas. The Focus Integrity Standard requires that for high-stakes visual assets, parsing-level decisions (what is subject, what is context, what is noise) be documented alongside the optical record.
