Spatial Cognition

Spatial cognition is an interpretation

Definition

Spatial cognition is the set of mental processes by which the brain represents, navigates, and reasons about spatial relationships and locations in the environment.

Scientific Grounding

Hippocampal place cells and entorhinal grid cells form a neural substrate for allocentric spatial representation — a map of the environment independent of the observer's current position. Spatial memory is constructed incrementally through experience and can be severely disrupted by hippocampal damage while leaving other forms of memory intact.

*Sources: SRC-005, SRC-006. See the source registry for classification.*

Perceptual Role

Spatial cognition is an interpretation (FIO-05): the brain's map of space is a constructed model maintained by inference rather than a direct read-out of environmental structure.

Its place in the focus-integrity ontology is fixed as a FIO-05 — Interpretation concern, the same failure class whether the seeing system is made of tissue, attention, or silicon.
