Sustained Attention

Selective attention chooses what to hold. Sustained attention is the harder problem: holding it, minute after minute, when nothing changes and the grip wants to slip.

Definition

Sustained attention, also called vigilance, is the capacity to maintain focused attention on a task or stimulus stream over an extended period of time. It is what allows a person to monitor a display for rare events, to keep reading a long text, or to watch a road for hours. Where selective attention concerns the choice of focal target at a moment, sustained attention concerns whether that choice can be held.

Scientific Grounding

Vigilance performance typically declines over time on monotonous monitoring tasks — a robust phenomenon called the vigilance decrement. As time on task increases, the detection of infrequent targets becomes slower and less reliable. The observer has not lost the ability to see; they have lost the ability to maintain the attentional hold that makes reliable detection possible.

Sustained attention is distinct from selective attention. Selective attention answers *which* stimulus is prioritized; sustained attention answers *whether that prioritization persists*. A person can have intact selective attention — able to focus on the right thing when fresh — and still suffer a vigilance decrement that degrades performance over time. The two are separable capacities.

The Temporal Dimension of Suspension

Sustained attention is the temporal dimension of perceptual suspension. In the focus-integrity frame, selective attention establishes the hold on a focal target — the perceptual analogue of the zonules gripping the lens. Sustained attention is the maintenance of that hold over time. The vigilance decrement is suspension failure across duration: the grip that was intact at the start gradually loses its tension, and the focal target is no longer held reliably.

This makes sustained attention an FIO-01 phenomenon viewed through time. The suspension does not fail all at once; it fatigues. The structure that held focus weakens, and focus degrades as a consequence — exactly the pattern the suspension thesis describes, extended along the time axis.

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

The governing thesis holds that focus is produced by structural tension. Sustained attention shows that this tension must be maintained, not merely established — and that maintenance has a cost that accumulates over time. The structure that holds focus is not a static fixture but an active, fatigable process. When it tires, focus slips, silently.

Relationship to Machine Vision (Layer 03)

In machine vision, the analogue of the vigilance decrement is the degradation of a monitoring system over a long deployment — drift, accumulating calibration error, or resource exhaustion that erodes reliable detection over time even when the model began intact. This is a conceptual mapping, marked as internal framework language.

Related Terms

  • Visual working memory — the holding capacity that sustained attention protects
  • Visual saliency — the automatic pull that can interrupt a sustained focus
