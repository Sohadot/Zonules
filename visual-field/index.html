Visual Field

The visual field is a provenance boundary

Definition

The visual field is the spatial extent of the environment that can be perceived when fixation is held steady, bounded by the anatomy of the retina and optic pathways.

Scientific Grounding

The central visual field is processed by the fovea and has the highest spatial resolution; peripheral regions are progressively less resolved. Defects in the visual field correspond to lesions at specific points along the visual pathway, making field mapping a tool for localizing pathology.

The World Health Organization's World Report on Vision lists glaucoma, which erodes the visual field, among the leading causes of irreversible vision impairment worldwide.

*Sources: SRC-001, SRC-003, SRC-026. See the source registry for classification.*

Structural Role

The visual field is a provenance boundary (FIO-04): it defines the scope of what the system can receive, and any region outside it is an unexamined source rather than a confirmed absence.

Layer Classification

Relationship to the Governing Thesis

Visual Field is one instance of the asset's governing claim: that focus is produced and held by structure that is not itself visible in the result. Its place in the focus-integrity ontology is fixed as a FIO-04 — Provenance concern, the same failure class whether the seeing system is made of tissue, attention, or silicon.

Mechanism

The visual field is the total area seen with the eyes held still, mapped onto the retina and preserved, in distorted form, through the visual pathway to the cortex.

Why It Matters

Loss of part of the visual field can go unnoticed because the brain completes across the gap, which is why field testing is central to detecting conditions such as glaucoma; the World Health Organization lists glaucoma among leading causes of irreversible impairment. In the thesis the visual field is a provenance concern: the system cannot establish what occupies a region it does not sample.

Failure Mode

Field defects are characteristically invisible to the person who has them, because the missing region is not seen as dark but is simply absent from awareness.

Common Misunderstanding

A blind spot or field loss is often imagined as a black patch; in fact the visual system fills across it, so the loss is silent rather than visible.

Human–Machine Bridge

A machine vision system has its own field of view and its own silent gaps beyond it, and like the eye it cannot report on what falls outside its sensor.
